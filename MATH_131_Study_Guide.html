Chapter 1: Statistical Basics Study Guide

Section 1.1: What is Statistics?

Statistics is the study of collecting, classifying, organizing, analyzing, and interpreting data. It includes two branches: descriptive statistics (collecting and organizing data) and inferential statistics (analyzing and interpreting data).

Key terms: Individual, Variable, Population (N), Sample, Parameter, Statistic, Qualitative, Quantitative (Discrete/Continuous), Measurement Scales (Nominal, Ordinal, Interval, Ratio).

  • Identify population, statistic, parameter, sample, and variable from given scenarios.
  • Classify data as qualitative, quantitative discrete, or quantitative continuous.
  • Determine measurement scales for different variables.

Section 1.2: Sampling Methods

Sampling is used when collecting data from an entire population is impractical. Methods include Simple Random, Systematic, Stratified, and Cluster sampling. Avoid convenience sampling due to bias. A census measures every individual.

  • Describe how to draw different types of samples (e.g., simple random, stratified).
  • Classify sampling techniques as random, stratified, systematic, cluster, or convenience.

Section 1.3: Experimental Design

Experimental design involves observational studies (watching/asking) and experiments (imposing treatments). Types include randomized two-treatment, randomized block, rigorously controlled, and matched pairs designs. Consider replication, blinding, and study types (cross-sectional, retrospective, prospective).

  • Identify if a study is observational or experimental.
  • Determine if an experiment is randomized or uses matched pairs.
  • Classify study types (cross-sectional, retrospective, prospective).

Section 1.4: How Not to Do Statistics

Avoid bias from funding, confounding variables, overgeneralization, and cause-effect misinterpretations. Watch for sampling errors, nonsampling errors, and survey biases (wording, order, non-response, voluntary response).

  • Identify bias or issues in given studies.
  • Explain cause-effect relationships or lack thereof.
  • Discuss survey question biases.
